Comparison of Joint Structures by Testing Bending Stiffness of Flexures

Betsy Li1, Neelima Sharma2, Madhusudhan Venkadesan2

1Science, Technology, and Research Scholar, Yale College, New Haven, CT 06520

2Department of Mechanical Engineering and Materials Science, Yale School of Engineering and Applied Science, CT 06520, USA

Biological joints vary widely in their morphology, and these variations are correlated with the function of the joint in its ecological setting, and with the size of the organism. The hypothesis underlying this work is that the variations in biological joints reflect various trade-offs between their passive stiffness, ability to impose movement constraints, and the range of rotation at these joints. A fundamental change in joint morphology is a switch from flexures to ligaments as the size of the animal increases. Flexural joints use a soft elastic beam that interconnects stiffer limb segments, while ligamentous joints use thin strips of ligaments that attach on the outside of the bone, and permit direct contact between the two articulating limb segments. Mathematical analysis of these two types of joints types suggested a size-dependent trade-off between the maximum range of rotation and mechanical stability. We therefore developed an experimental setup to measure the stiffness and range of rotation in physical models of these two joint morphologies. This paper focuses on the design of the mechanical testing setup as well as the design and fabrication of physical representations of the two biological joint types. An experiment was designed to measure the bending stiffness by collecting data for torque and angular deflection in order to determine the range of rotation of a muscle. The experiment utilized optomechanical components (ThorLabs Inc.), a high-resolution 6-axis force/torque sensor (JR3 Inc.), 3D motion capture (Vicon Inc.), custom designed joints, and custom designed loading rigs. Measurements included the force, motion, location, and angle displaced as the joint was pulled on indirectly using a single-axis translation stage. The resulting set up allowed collection of all necessary variables, with little friction. This is ongoing work in the lab, and expected to generate data will be able to better guide engineers in the process of designing robotic joints and general devices based on the range of motion of interest as well as help achieve a better understanding of the body structures for all types of species.
